Data Cleaning & Preprocessing
Fixing TotalCharges (stored as object with blank strings for new customers), converting SeniorCitizen from int to readable labels, and verifying zero nulls.
# Fix TotalCharges — blank = tenure 0, no charges df["TotalCharges"] = df["TotalCharges"].replace(" ", 0) df["TotalCharges"] = df["TotalCharges"].astype("float") # Convert SeniorCitizen 0/1 → readable yes/no def conv(value): return "yes" if value == 1 else "no" df['SeniorCitizen'] = df["SeniorCitizen"].apply(conv) # Verify — no nulls, no duplicates print(df.isnull().sum().sum()) # → 0 print(df.duplicated().sum()) # → 0
Output
Null values : 0 Duplicates : 0 TotalCharges dtype: float64 ✓
✅ Clean dataset: Zero null values, zero duplicates. TotalCharges correctly cast to float. Dataset is ready for EDA.
Churn Distribution
How many customers churned vs stayed? The pie chart and count plot reveal the class imbalance in the target variable.
ax = sns.countplot(x='Churn', data=df) ax.bar_label(ax.containers[0]) plt.title("Count of Customers by Churn") plt.show()
📊 26.54% of customers (1,869 out of 7,043) have churned. The remaining 73.46% (5,174) are retained. This class imbalance is important to account for in any predictive model.
Churn by Gender
Does gender influence churn behaviour? Comparing male vs female churn rates to identify demographic patterns.
sns.countplot(x="gender", data=df, hue="Churn") plt.title("Churn by Gender") plt.show()
⚖️ Gender is not a significant predictor. Churn rates are nearly equal — Female: ~27.4%, Male: ~26.2%. Gender-based retention strategies would not be effective.
Senior Citizen Churn Analysis
Senior citizens (65+) represent a minority but show a disproportionately high churn rate — a key business segment to address.
count_data = pd.crosstab(df['SeniorCitizen'], df['Churn']) count_data_pct = count_data / count_data.sum().sum() * 100 count_data_pct.plot(kind='bar', stacked=True) plt.show()
👴 Senior citizens churn at ~42% vs ~24% for non-seniors. Despite making up only 16.2% of the customer base, they are a high-risk segment needing targeted retention programs.
Tenure vs Churn
Tenure (months with the company) is one of the strongest predictors of churn. Early months are the highest-risk window.
sns.histplot(x="tenure", data=df, bins=50, hue="Churn") plt.show()
⏱️ Customers in their first 1–2 months churn the most. After 12+ months, churn drops sharply. Long-term customers (60+ months) are the most loyal. Early engagement programs are critical.
Contract Type Impact
Contract duration is one of the most powerful churn predictors. Month-to-month customers churn at dramatically higher rates.
ax = sns.countplot(x='Contract', data=df, hue="Churn") ax.bar_label(ax.containers[0]) plt.title("Count of Customer by Contract") plt.show()
📄 Month-to-month contracts have a ~43% churn rate vs ~11% for one-year and ~3% for two-year contracts. Migrating customers to longer contracts is the single biggest retention lever.
Payment Method Analysis
How customers pay correlates strongly with churn — electronic check users are significantly more likely to leave.
ax = sns.countplot(x='PaymentMethod', data=df, hue="Churn") plt.xticks(rotation=45) plt.title("Count by PaymentMethod") plt.show()
💳 Electronic check users churn at ~45% — far above credit card (~15%), bank transfer (~16%), and mailed check (~19%). Auto-payment adoption campaigns could significantly reduce churn.
Service Feature Analysis (3×3 Grid)
Exploring 9 service features — OnlineSecurity, TechSupport, StreamingTV, and more — to see which correlate with higher churn.
columns = ['PhoneService', 'MultipleLines', 'InternetService', 'OnlineSecurity', 'OnlineBackup', 'DeviceProtection', 'TechSupport', 'StreamingTV', 'StreamingMovies'] fig, axes = plt.subplots(3, 3, figsize=(15,12)) for i, col in enumerate(columns): sns.countplot(x=col, data=df, ax=axes[i//3][i%3], hue="Churn") plt.show()
🔒 Customers without OnlineSecurity and TechSupport churn at ~2× the rate of those who have them. Fiber optic internet users (vs DSL) also show higher churn — possibly due to price sensitivity.
